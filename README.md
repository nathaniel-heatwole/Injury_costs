SUMMARY

• Injury severity and economic cost modeling

• Published in: Journal of Safety Research (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022437526000034)

• Developed cost curves for the Injury Severity Scale, which is ubiquitous in injury research (12,000+ Google Scholar citations)

• Methodology can be applied to any injury quantity (not just costs), including: incidence, ED admission, hospitalization rate, length of stay, ICU admission, mortality rate, work days lost, and disability/impairment

MAJOR COMPONENTS

• Bounded feasible region, created probabilistic map, transferred existing cost data, and fit regression-based predictive and link models

• Collected empirical injury data (incidence/prevalence, hospitalization rate, mortality risk, and economic costs)

• Critically assessed data quality across sources, considering abundancy (study size) and recency (years covered)

• Cost data encompass: cost-of-injury (medical treatment and work lost), quality-related costs (e.g., pain and suffering), and willingness-to-pay estimates (based on hedonic wage-risk studies)

• Clustered ranges of injury severity values according to various data features, including the newly mapped cost values

IMPLICATIONS

• Reduced-form linear cost functions and link models are now available

• Allows seemingly disparate injury severity scales to be better and more directly compared and facilitates the pooling of mixed data in meta-analyses

• Derived interesting insights relating to the various injury severity scales, their relationship to one another, and how they should be applied in analyses

TOOLS USED

• Python, econometrics, operations research, logistic regression, clustering (k-means), hypothesis testing (likelihood ratio test)
